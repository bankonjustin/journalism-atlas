# James Design Principles
*James Bareham / Happicamp — Web Design Principles for Journalism Atlas*
*Source: james-design-principles.pdf*

---

## 1. The Tool Principle
**A website is a tool, not an experience.**

Every design decision serves legibility and utility first. Visual interest is always in service of the tool, never decorative. This does not mean the design cannot be engaging, beautiful, or distinctive — it means that engagement, beauty, and distinctiveness must earn their place by making the tool work better for the user.

If a design element cannot answer the question "how does this help the user do what they came here to do?" it does not belong on the page.

---

## 2. The Mirror Principle
**A homepage should be a mirror of the user, not a statement about the product.**

The first thing a user encounters should reflect back the range of reasons someone might be here — and give them enough context to recognize themselves in one of those options. Routing is the mechanism, but the design must do the work of illuminating the choices, not just presenting them.

Users don't always know what they're looking for. It is the website's job to show them. Never assume self-knowledge on the part of the user. Never present a choice without enough context to make it meaningful.

---

## 3. The Explanation Principle
**Explain, don't assume.**

Every data module, every section, every new surface needs a minimal narrative entry point — one or two sentences that tell the user what they're looking at and why it matters. A website never assumes the user will instinctively understand what they're seeing.

This applies globally. Every module on every page should be able to answer: what is this, and why should I care? If it can't, it needs a line of copy before the content starts.

Brevity here does not mean fewer words — it means focus and clarity. One precise sentence beats three vague ones.

---

## 4. The Surface Hierarchy Principle
**Dark and light surfaces have distinct and non-interchangeable jobs.**

**Dark/black surfaces** — reserved for: big bold headlines, punchy thesis statements, visual data, animated elements, vibrant infographics, featured editorial content, CTAs that need to pop. Never for information-dense text. Dark surfaces create impact and prominence. They are a **stage**.

**White/light gray surfaces** — reserved for: data, information, readable text, cards, filters, database content. This is where the user does the actual work. White surfaces create legibility and trust. They are a **workspace**.

Transitions are intentional, not decorative. Alternating dark/light/dark/light without reason is visually exhausting and creates friction. The page should have a deliberate rhythm. Return to dark only when there is a specific reason — a featured module, a CTA block, a visual hero. Not as a pattern for its own sake.

**The stage/workspace model:** A dark module elevates and features content, giving it drama and weight. Clicking through takes the user to a white destination where the work happens. These are two distinct modes and the design should make the transition feel natural and intentional.

---

## 5. The Brevity Principle
**Brevity means focus and clarity, not just fewer words.**

There are two kinds of brevity in web design:

**Structural brevity** — fewer pages, fewer nav items, fewer modules on a page. Go slim. Fewer surfaces with more intentional paths outperform more surfaces with diluted purpose.

**Editorial brevity** — copy that is precise, not just short. A headline that completes its thought is better than a clever incomplete one. A module intro that tells the user exactly what they're looking at is better than a stylish one that leaves them guessing.

Both kinds matter. Neither is sufficient without the other.

---

## 6. The Scale Principle
**Design for double the current inventory from day one.**

Every repeating element — navigation items, card grids, partner lists, content modules — is designed to handle twice the current volume without redesign. Growth should be a content operation, not a design or development task.

If you have 10 items today, the template handles 20 comfortably. If you have 30, design for 60. A system that requires redesign to accommodate growth was under-designed from the start.

This principle applies to: navigation items, card and grid layouts, partner or client lists, content index pages, any repeating data structure.

---

## 7. The Template Principle
**The system must absorb new content without one-off work.**

Templates, not bespoke pages. Every new piece of content that arrives — a new partner, a new case study, a new team member, a new research output — should slot into an existing template without requiring a design or development decision.

When a new content type requires a bespoke page, that is a signal that the template system is incomplete — not that the content is special.

---

## 8. The Version Control Principle
**Two states only: draft or live.**

There is no third state. No iteration files, no numbered versions, no "v3-finalFINAL." One working file per page or component. One live file. When the working file meets the minimum viable standard it becomes live. Everything else gets archived or deleted.

This applies to every deliverable: pages, components, copy documents, design files. Multiple versions of an edit file is a maintenance nightmare and creates confusion about what the current truth is.

Think of it like a feature article: it's in edits until it's ready to publish.

---

## 9. The Mobile Principle
**Mobile-first, not mobile-equal.**

Design for the smallest screen first. Desktop is an enhancement, not the primary canvas. If a design does not work at 390px, it does not work.

Mobile-first also enforces the brevity discipline that every project needs. If a module cannot be explained, navigated, and used on a phone screen, it is probably too complex for any screen.

Assume the majority of users are on mobile. Design accordingly.

---

## 10. The Navigation Principle
**Navigation has two distinct jobs that must not be conflated.**

**Information navigation** — tells the user about the organization, the project, the methodology, the team. Audience-agnostic. Lives in the persistent header. Answers: who built this and why should I trust it?

**Tool navigation** — helps the user find and understand what they're looking at within a specific product surface. Contextual. Lives on the page, not in the header. Answers: where am I in this tool and where can I go from here?

Mixing these two navigation types in the same header creates an overcrowded, undifferentiated nav that serves neither job well. Separate the jobs. Slim the header.

---

## 11. The CTA Principle
**Calls to action are not navigation.**

A CTA asks the user to do something — submit, sign up, contact, apply. Navigation takes the user somewhere. These are different jobs and require different design treatment.

CTAs do not belong in the primary navigation alongside informational links. They belong:
- On the pages where the action is contextually relevant
- As persistent footer links for always-available actions
- As buttons with distinct visual treatment that differentiates them from nav items

When a CTA drifts into the nav it loses its urgency and the nav loses its clarity.

---

## 12. The Transparency Principle
**Users should never be surprised.**

This applies to paywalls, content gates, data limitations, and any other boundary the user might encounter. If something requires registration, payment, or a commercial relationship to access, that fact is communicated clearly and early — as a design element, not a footnote.

The right model: show the shape of what exists, gate the detail. The user always knows what is there and what accessing it requires. They are never surprised by a wall mid-journey.

A threshold, not a wall. The user arrives at any gate feeling informed and interested, not blocked and frustrated.

---

## 13. The Placeholder Principle
**Never show empty states in public-facing design.**

No "coming soon" slots. No placeholder cards. No empty grid cells. No "partner slot" boxes. Either the content exists and is shown, or it does not appear at all.

Placeholders signal incompleteness to the user even when the intent is to signal growth. A clean list of three confirmed items reads as more authoritative than a list of three confirmed items and three empty boxes.

---

## 14. The Hierarchy Principle
**Public rankings create editorial problems. Internal operational designations do not.**

When content needs to be prioritized or featured, the basis for that prioritization should be operational — asset readiness, data completeness, relevance to current context — not a public quality ranking.

Public rankings invite challenges, complaints, and the question "who decided this?" Internal operational designations are invisible to the user and serve the product without creating a narrative about hierarchy.

Feature content because it is ready and relevant. Never because it is "best."

---

## 15. The Two-Way Mirror Principle
**Every standalone content destination should be a two-way entry point.**

A page that can be reached from social media or search — an editorial feature, a research paper, a case study — should always carry a clear bridge back to the main product or tool. A user who arrives from outside should never feel stranded. They should always know there is something larger behind what they're reading.

Equally, the main product should always surface links to standalone content destinations where relevant. The relationship is bidirectional: product → content and content → product.

This creates a self-reinforcing discovery loop that serves both new users and returning users.

---

## The Overriding Test

Before any design decision is finalized, apply this test:

1. Does it help the user do what they came here to do? If not, it does not belong.
2. Does it explain itself, or does it assume the user already understands? If it assumes, add copy.
3. Is it on the right surface? Impact and headlines on dark. Information and data on white.
4. Will it scale to double the current volume without redesign? If not, redesign the template.
5. Is it a tool or a decoration? If decoration, remove it.
